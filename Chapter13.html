Chapter 13 — Project Evaluation and Control
BMGT 300 · Control Cycles, EVM, and Human Factors
Why control?
Project constraints
Projects are defined by budget and schedule limitations. Monitoring and control keep the team on top of evolving status across all life cycle stages.
Core model
The control cycle
1. Set a goal (baseline). 2. Measure progress. 3. Compare actual vs. planned. 4. Take corrective action.
Key methods
Evaluation tools
Milestone analysis, S‑curves, and Earned Value Management (EVM) are the primary techniques for tracking schedule and cost performance.
Human side
Behavioral factors
Success depends on coordination, project structure, uniqueness, budget pressure, and avoiding initial over‑optimism.

The four‑step control cycle
1
Set a goal
Go beyond scope; set the project baseline plan (WBS, schedule, budget).
2
Measure progress
Use real‑time systems to collect ongoing status data (on‑time information).
3
Compare actual vs. planned
Gap analysis – determine how much performance deviates from goals. S‑curves show cumulative budget vs. actual cost over time.
4
Take action
If significant deviation is detected, apply corrective action to bring the project back on track.
S‑Curve insight
An S‑curve plots cumulative cost (budgeted vs. actual) against time. A negative variance occurs when the actual cost curve lies above the planned value curve, indicating overspending.
Milestone analysis
Benefit 1
Signal completion
Milestones serve as a common language to mark important accomplishments.
Benefit 2
Motivate the team
Recognising successes helps keep the project team engaged.
Benefit 3
Reevaluation points
“Stop points” to review progress, accept change requests, and realign with stakeholders.
Benefit 4
Coordinate schedules
Help vendors and suppliers avoid delays by synchronising deliveries.
Additional uses
Function
Key review gates
Mandatory mid‑term reviews in complex projects (e.g., US government projects).
Function
Signal team members
Alert non‑core members (quality assurance, inspectors) when their work is about to start.
Function
Delineate work packages
Refocus effort and resources on troubled deliverables rather than in a general way.
Earned Value Management – key terms
Term Formula / Description
Planned Value (PV) Budgeted cost of work scheduled – the baseline at a point in time.
Earned Value (EV) Budgeted cost of work performed so far.
Actual Cost (AC) Real cost of work performed to date.
Schedule Variance (SV) SV = EV − PV – positive means ahead of schedule.
Schedule Performance Index (SPI) SPI = EV ÷ PV – >1 indicates ahead of schedule.
Cost Variance (CV) CV = EV − AC – negative means over budget.
Cost Performance Index (CPI) CPI = EV ÷ AC – <1 indicates cost overrun.
Budget at Completion (BAC) Total approved budget for the project.
Estimate at Completion (EAC) EAC = BAC ÷ CPI or EAC = AC + (BAC − EV)
Steps in EVM 1. Define activities & budget. 2. Schedule resources. 3. Time‑phased budget (PV). 4. Total actual costs (AC). 5. Calculate SV & CV.
Earned Value example (7‑month project, BAC = $118 k)
At a certain point:
• PV = 103 k
• EV = 44 k
• AC = 78 k

→ SV = 44 − 103 = −59 k (behind schedule)
→ CV = 44 − 78 = −34 k (over budget)
→ SPI = 44/103 ≈ 0.43 (work progressing at 43 % of planned pace)
→ CPI = 44/78 ≈ 0.56 (getting 56 ¢ of value for every dollar spent)
Human factors that influence control success
Factor 1
Stakeholder coordination
Quality of relationships among team members, sponsors, and clients.
Factor 2
Project structure & control
Adequacy of monitoring systems and clear authority lines.
Factor 3
Uniqueness & public exposure
Novel or high‑visibility projects demand more rigorous evaluation.
Factor 4
Success criteria consensus
Stakeholders must agree on what “done” and “success” look like.
Factor 5
Budgetary pressure
Too little pressure may lead to lax control; too much can create dysfunctional behavior.
Factor 6
Initial over‑optimism
Avoid unrealistic early estimates – they set an unachievable baseline.
