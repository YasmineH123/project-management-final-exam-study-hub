Chapter 8 — Cost Estimation and Budgeting
BMGT 300 · Project Management: Achieving Competitive Advantage
Core definition
Cost management
Encompasses data collection, cost accounting, and cost control. Cost accounting and cost control serve as the chief mechanisms for identifying and maintaining control over project costs.
Core definition
Project budget
A plan that identifies the resources, goals, and schedule that allows a firm to achieve those goals. Cost estimation creates a reasonable budget baseline.
Common sources of project cost
5 major sources
1. Labor — personnel, salary, hourly rate
2. Materials — based on industry
3. Subcontractors — consultants, marketing, PR
4. Equipment & facilities — away from HQ
5. Travel — hotels, car rentals
Key concept
Budget contingencies
Extra funds to cover uncertainties. Needed because: scope may change, Murphy's Law applies, interaction costs must be anticipated, and normal conditions are rarely encountered.

The 4 pairs of cost classifications
↗
Direct Costs
Directly related to revenue generated — mainly labor and material. E.g., personnel building an airport.
↘
Indirect Costs
Not allocated directly. E.g., support personnel (cost accountant). Includes overhead (taxes, utilities, insurance) and SG&A (advertising, salaries, commissions).
↻
Recurring Costs
Continue over the project's life cycle. E.g., labor, material, logistics, sales costs.
⚡
Nonrecurring Costs
Charged once at beginning or end. E.g., personnel training, preliminary marketing analysis.
📌
Fixed Costs
Don't change with usage. E.g., leased equipment, project hardware, rental cost.
📊
Variable Costs
Directly proportional to usage level. E.g., hourly labor, sales commission, equipment leased per hour.
✓
Normal Costs
Incurred to complete a project as per schedule — planned, expected costs.
⚡
Expedited Costs
Unplanned costs to speed up completion. E.g., crashing activities — overtime, hiring temporary workers.
Project price breakdown (Figure 8.2)
Total Project Price = Direct Costs + Indirect Costs + Gross Margin
Indirect costs include overhead and SG&A. Gross margin is the profit markup.
Cost estimation methods
Method 1
Ballpark estimates (order of magnitude)
Used when time or info is scarce. Accuracy ±30%. Should not substitute for informed cost estimation. E.g., client asks for a bid with a very short deadline.
Method 2
Comparative estimates
Historical data used as reference for similar projects. Boeing uses parametric estimation — detailed estimates with a multiplier for inflation, labor, material increases.
Method 3
Feasibility estimates
Based on real numbers after completing preliminary project design work. Includes requesting quotes from suppliers and subcontractors.
Method 4
Definitive estimates
Only after completing most design work. Scope is well understood. Purchase orders submitted, prices and availabilities well known. Best accuracy.
Software project estimation — Function Points
Definition
Function Points (FP)
A standard unit of measure representing the functional size of a software application — the number of functions the software delivers to users.
Process
Function Point Analysis
A system for estimating the size of software projects based on what the software does. Uses a complexity weighting table to score inputs, outputs, inquiries, files, and interfaces.
Budgeting procedures
Approach 1
Top-Down Budgeting
Senior management sets the overall budget and allocates it downward. Quick but may miss ground-level details. Based on high-level project scope.
Approach 2
Bottom-Up Budgeting
Individual work packages are estimated first, then rolled up into the total project budget. More accurate but time-consuming. Uses the WBS as a foundation.
Approach 3
Activity-Based Costing (ABC)
Projects use activities → activities use resources. 4 steps: (1) Assign costs to activities, (2) Identify cost drivers, (3) Compute cost rate per driver unit, (4) Multiply rate × volume.
Approach 4
Time-Phased Budgeting
Distributes the budget over the project timeline. Allows tracking planned vs. actual costs at any point. Creates a cumulative budgeted cost curve for progress monitoring.
Activity-Based Costing — step by step
ABC Formula
Total Activity Cost = Cost Driver Rate × Volume of Cost Driver Units
Example: If labor is the cost driver and the rate is $75/hour, and the project uses 200 hours → Labor Cost = $75 × 200 = $15,000
Budget contingencies
The allocation of extra funds to cover uncertainties and improve the chance of finishing on time. Contingencies are needed because:

• Project scope may change
• Murphy's Law — if something can go wrong, it often will
• Cost estimation must anticipate interaction costs
• Normal conditions are rarely encountered
WBS → Scheduling → Budgeting relationship
The WBS identifies project tasks → Scheduling assigns time to those tasks → Budgeting assigns costs. Together they form the project's baseline plan. Time-phased budgets combine schedule and cost data for earned value tracking.
