Chapter 7 — Risk Management
BMGT 300 · Project Management: Achieving Competitive Advantage
Core definition
Risk management
The art and science of identifying, analyzing, and responding to risk factors throughout the life of a project, in the best interest of its objectives.
Core definition
Project risk
An uncertain event or condition that, if it occurs, has a positive or negative effect on one or more project objectives — scope, schedule, cost, or quality.
Key questions in risk management
4 questions to always ask
1. What is likely to happen? (probability & impact)
2. What can minimize probability or impact?
3. What cues will signal the need for action?
4. What are the likely outcomes of problems?
Risk clusters
Types of risks
Financial · Technical · Commercial · Execution · Contractual/legal

Common examples: absenteeism, resignation, staff pulled away, time overruns, skills unavailable, incomplete specs, change orders.
Risk factor identification methods
Method 1
Brainstorming meetings
Group sessions to surface potential risks through open discussion among project team members.
Method 2
Expert opinion
Leveraging experienced individuals who have faced similar risks in past projects.
Method 3
Past history
Drawing on lessons learned, documentation, and outcomes from previous projects.
Method 4
Multiple / team-based assessments
Using consensus from the project team to balance individual biases and blind spots.
Risk mitigation strategies
Strategy When to use Example
Accept Small probability and minor consequences Acknowledge and monitor only
Minimize Risk can be reduced through action Boeing minimizing vendor quality risk
Share Risk spread across multiple parties European Space Agency & Airbus consortium
Transfer Move financial burden to another party Fixed-price contracts; liquidated damages clauses
Insurance Transfer financial obligations to insurer Common in construction industry

The 4 stages of risk management
1
Risk identification
Identify all possible risks using brainstorming, expert opinion, past history, and team assessments. Use a Risk Breakdown Structure (RBS) to organize risk categories hierarchically.
2
Analysis of probability and consequences
Assess how likely each risk is (probability of failure) and how severe the impact would be (consequence of failure). Use a Risk Impact Matrix to visualize likelihood vs. severity.
3
Risk mitigation strategies
Choose a response: Accept · Minimize · Share · Transfer · Insurance. Strategy depends on probability level and consequence severity.
4
Control and documentation
Classify and codify risks, responses, and outcomes. A change management report answers: What (source)? Who (responsible)? When (mitigation)? Why (reasons)? How (plan)?

Project risk scoring formula
Probability of failure (Pf)
Pf = (Pm + Pc + Pd) / 3
Pm = Maturity  ·  Pc = Complexity  ·  Pd = Dependency
Consequence of failure (Cf)
Cf = (Cc + Cs + Cr + Cp) / 4
Cc = Cost  ·  Cs = Schedule  ·  Cr = Reliability  ·  Cp = Performance
Overall risk factor (RF)
RF = Pf + Cf − (Pf × Cf)
Risk thresholds (rule of thumb)
Low risk
RF < 0.30
Proceed normally; standard monitoring sufficient.
Medium risk
RF = 0.30 – 0.70
Active mitigation strategies required; regular review.
High risk
RF > 0.70
Significant action needed; consider restructuring the project.
Example walkthrough (software product)
Probability contributors: (1) Maturity — is it new or based on existing platform? (2) Complexity — simple or highly complex structure? (3) Dependency — can it be developed independently?

Consequence contributors: (1) Cost — budget adherence vs overruns. (2) Schedule — on time vs severe delays. (3) Reliability — quality/usefulness of finished product. (4) Performance — how well the software performs its designed functions.
